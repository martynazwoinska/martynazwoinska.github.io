# Bristol N2 location research and art brief

Last updated: 2026-07-21

This is the first location dossier in the location-by-location Game of Worms illustration workflow. It is the evidence and review contract for the Bristol N2 landscape and its three accessory pairs. Later locations require their own dossier before their artwork is revised.

## Evidence boundaries

### Exact collection record

- Isotype: N2, *Caenorhabditis elegans*.
- Sampling date recorded by CaeNDR: 1951-01-01.
- Coordinates: 51.45, -2.59.
- Landscape: urban garden.
- Substrate: compost.
- Sampled by: W. Nicholas.
- Primary catalogue source: [CaeNDR N2 isotype record](https://caendr.org/isotype/N2/).

The illustration must show compost as the immediate microhabitat and an urban garden as its enclosing setting. CaeNDR does not identify the Avon Gorge as the collection point. The gorge and bridge may locate the scene as Bristol context, but must not replace or obscure the recorded garden and compost.

The current website copy says “mushroom compost.” The structured CaeNDR record supports “compost” but does not supply the modifier “mushroom.” Preserve approved public copy during this art-only batch, but do not add mushroom-industry machinery or other unsupported collection details to the drawing.

### Regional landscape and landmark context

- Bristol City Council’s River Avon heritage assessment describes iconic views along the Avon Gorge, a wooded or rural character on one side, urban terraces on the other, and the Clifton Suspension Bridge as a defining structure.
- The Clifton Suspension Bridge Trust describes the bridge as spanning the Avon Gorge and as an enduring symbol of Bristol.
- The Trust’s site guide identifies 26 m towers built from local Pennant sandstone with Bath stone near the top, plus iron suspension chains and rods.
- The Avon Gorge is a steep, geologically exposed river corridor with wooded faces and a protected natural history. These cues distinguish it from a generic green valley.

### Local-object candidates for a later accessory decision

N2 keeps its approved laboratory trio in this batch. The research nevertheless identified Bristol objects and material cues that could support a different future concept without inventing generic “local dress”:

- **Suspension-chain engineering model:** the bridge’s wrought-iron chain links, vertical rods and distinctive tower openings could become a small mechanical instrument or articulated prop. Use the structure as factual inspiration, not a copied Trust illustration.
- **Stone and iron material study:** the Clifton-side tower guide identifies local Pennant sandstone, Bath stone near the tower tops and painted cast-iron caps. These materials could inform a sample case, geology lens or construction toy with clearly different functions and silhouettes.
- **Camera obscura optic:** the Bridge Trust’s Leigh Woods guide identifies the camera obscura in the Clifton Observatory. A small optical viewer or rotating lens would be locally grounded and visually unlike the Game’s existing monocles and telescopes.
- **“Bristol Diamonds” geology specimen:** Bristol City Council’s Clifton Down history trail discusses quartz geodes historically associated with St Vincent’s Rock. A sealed specimen viewer could reference that history. It must not encourage collecting rock from a protected site.
- **Rare gorge flora as a non-collecting motif:** official Bristol material highlights autumn squill, Bristol onion and other protected plants. They may inform a drawn pattern or observation card, but should not be shown picked, worn as harvested flowers or removed from the gorge.
- **Urban-garden compost tools:** a compost scoop, aerator or small garden sieve would connect to the recorded substrate more directly than a city landmark, but would need a source-backed historical or contemporary Bristol form before being treated as locally distinctive.

## Landscape composition contract

The scene must remain legible at thumbnail size and use three linked depths rather than a row of interchangeable symbols.

### Foreground: recorded microhabitat

- A recognisable, irregular compost heap with layered decomposing leaves and garden material.
- Garden soil, leaf litter, a modest brick or timber edge, and restrained planting cues establish an urban garden.
- Compost must be visually closer and more prominent than the bridge.
- Avoid a pristine flower bed, tropical fruit grove, laboratory bench, or generic park lawn.

### Middle ground: Avon Gorge context

- A narrow tidal River Avon channel recedes through two unequal gorge faces.
- At low tide, a narrow, gently bending water channel sits within a broader muted riverbed. Its lower reach passes behind the near garden edge rather than terminating visibly or continuing as a road-like wedge to the bottom of the frame. Avoid a centred triangular wedge, straight parallel banks or repeated full-width ripples that make the water resemble a road or appear to climb the gorge.
- One face should read as more wooded; the other should expose tilted limestone or rock strata and urban-edge cues.
- The river, cliff geometry, vegetation and built edge must form one coherent view rather than separate clip-art objects.

### Background: Bristol lock silhouette

- Clifton Suspension Bridge crosses the gorge with two distinct masonry towers, a level deck, curved suspension chains and vertical rods.
- The bridge itself provides the Bristol urban lock at small sizes. Omit secondary terrace symbols when they compete with the tower or read as a generic skyline.
- The locked silhouette is the bridge spanning opposing gorge faces over a central river notch. If this is not recognisable when small, the composition has failed.

### Palette and rendering

- Use the Game’s existing restrained emerald, antique-gold, muted-violet or berry system, with scientifically meaningful agar and cryogenic colours retained.
- Vary line weight and shape detail by depth; foreground forms may carry texture, while the background remains quieter.
- Avoid photorealism, generic gradients, glow, or a collection of repeated tree, rock and building primitives.

## Accessory evidence and design contract

The three concepts are scientific play objects, not claims about Bristol dress or local material culture. They are retained because they explain why N2 is central to laboratory nematode research.

### Seeded NGM agar plates

WormBook states that *C. elegans* is maintained on Nematode Growth Medium agar in Petri plates and is usually fed *E. coli* OP50. It describes a limited, central OP50 lawn because this makes worms easier to observe.

Source: [Maintenance of C. elegans, WormBook/NCBI Bookshelf](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK19649/), sections 3.1–3.3.

Required visible construction:

- clear dish base, rim and lid or glare;
- pale NGM agar;
- a central, bounded OP50 lawn rather than colour covering the whole plate;
- subtle worm tracks or worm forms;
- restrained N2/OP50 labelling where it remains legible;
- primary and companion dishes with different silhouettes and colony patterns;
- placement as a held shield, tray or halo beside the body, never as a hat.

### Fitted lab coats

The lab coat is a playful research cue rather than a locally sourced Bristol object. Its quality is judged by tailoring to the two actual worm curves.

Required visible construction:

- separate collars, opening, seams, pockets and fasteners;
- a badge or pen detail where scale permits;
- primary: longer, belted coat following the large worm’s curve;
- companion: shorter asymmetric coat specifically fitted to the smaller worm;
- face, eyes and mouth unobscured;
- no boxy generic garment transformed into two sizes.

### Cryo-vial jetpacks

WormBook describes labelled cryovials, gradual cooling to -80 °C and long-term storage in liquid nitrogen. The jetpack is an imaginative translation of those real storage objects; it must not imply that live worms literally fly using cryogenic equipment.

Source: [Maintenance of C. elegans, WormBook/NCBI Bookshelf](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK19649/), section 7.

Required visible construction:

- recognisable cryovial bodies, threaded-looking caps, frozen medium and labels;
- fitted harness or bracket so the object reads as attached equipment;
- restrained ice, snow or cold-plume cues;
- primary: twin-vial assembly with a coherent shell or rack;
- companion: a single main vial with two different side nozzles or stabilisers;
- no ordinary backpack silhouette and no generic flame thrusters.

## Quality benchmark

Commit `c603884` is the minimum historical benchmark, not the target. It supplied layered plate construction, body-following coats and detailed cryo packs. The revised N2 set must improve on it through clearer material construction, better fit to each worm, deliberately different pair geometry, cleaner combined layering and a stronger landscape composition.

## Review and interaction acceptance

- Only the Bristol N2 landscape and N2 accessory drawings change in this batch.
- Each primary/companion pair is visibly different in outline, proportions and internal construction.
- All three accessory concepts can be shown together without hiding faces or collapsing into an unreadable pile.
- Each visible copy remains directly draggable and keyboard movable.
- Moving the primary copy never moves the companion copy, and vice versa.
- Position state remains separate per worm, species and location for the session.
- Home resets only the focused accessory copy.
- Accessible names describe the visible objects.
- Pointer and keyboard checks cover each of the six N2 objects.
- Visual checks cover light and dark themes at 360, 390, 768, 1024, 1120, 1235 and 1440 px where the layout changes.
- No horizontal overflow, console error, broken source link or change to another location is accepted.
- `git diff --check`, source audits and the full diff pass before integration.
