Tiny surpriseN2 came from mushroom compost near Bristol. C. elegans can be frozen alive in a protective solution and revived after thawing. This is the reason for its cryo-vial jetpack. An early N2 tube frozen around 1968 was later thawed to establish an ancestral laboratory stock.

For curious grown-ups: map notes and sources

Markers show selected published collection records, not complete species ranges. Dense sampling areas are represented by a few readable points. The illustrated landscapes teach the wider regional setting around each record; they do not claim that a worm was collected from every feature shown. The “Place clues” source supports each regional scene. “Mostly selfing” means self-fertile hermaphrodites predominate, although rare males and occasional outcrossing can occur.

  • CaeNDR strain database and Crombie et al. (2024): coordinates and ecological metadata for representative C. elegans, C. briggsae, and C. tropicalis records.
  • Félix Lab strain database: collection substrates and locations for C. inopinata, C. nigoni, and C. wallacei.
  • CGC N2 record and Sterken et al. (2015): the Bristol N2 strain's journey from mushroom compost to the canonical laboratory reference.
  • Kiontke et al. (2011): Caenorhabditis phylogeny, habitats, and tropical/temperate distributions.
  • Félix et al. (2014): species diagnoses for C. nigoni, C. tropicalis, and C. wallacei.
  • Stevens et al. (2019): comparative phylogeny and repeated evolution of self-fertilization in the Elegans group.
  • Woodruff & Phillips (2018): C. inopinata in Okinawan figs and fig wasps.
  • Bi et al. (2019): the C. briggsae–C. nigoni pair for studying speciation genetics.
  • Hernández-Sánchez et al. (2025): C. tropicalis and its sister species C. wallacei.
  • Official Okinawa travel guide: the five-and-four kasuri motif of Yaeyama minsā cloth made on Ishigaki and Taketomi.
  • University of Hawaiʻi Kawaihāpai: lauhala weaving from prepared pandanus leaves.
  • Noble et al. (2021): global diversity and frequent selfing in C. tropicalis.
  • Wang et al. (2026 preprint): global sampling and geographic diversity in C. tropicalis.
  • Barrière & Félix (2005): predominant selfing and rare outcrossing in wild C. elegans.
