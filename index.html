About

Overview

I am an evolutionary biologist in the Animal Ecology group at the Department of Ecology and Genetics, Uppsala University, where I work within the Lind Lab. My research asks how selection shapes life histories over the lifespan — why organisms age the way they do, how they trade current reproduction against survival and future reproduction, and how information about the environment is used, and passed between generations, to guide these decisions.

I combine experimental evolution, work in model systems such as Drosophila and the nematode Caenorhabditis elegans, and evolutionary modelling. I earned my PhD from Uppsala University in 2017 with a thesis on age-specific trade-offs in life-history evolution, and my work spans cognitive ageing, sexual selection and conflict, transgenerational effects, and evolutionary genetics.

Research

Research interests

My central question is how organisms adapt to environments that change over time — through genetic evolution, developmental and reversible plasticity, parental effects, and bet-hedging — and how features of their biology, such as whether they reproduce by selfing or outcrossing, tip the balance between these strategies.

Current work

Adaptive response modes under environmental uncertainty

Reproductive mode and the evolution of adaptive responses

In an ongoing modelling project I ask how reproductive mode — especially selfing versus outcrossing — changes the adaptive responses populations evolve when the environment is uncertain. Using individual-based population-genetic simulations, I compare the response strategies available to organisms along several axes:

GaAdaptive tracking
GdevDevelopmental plasticity
GrevReversible plasticity
GpParental information
σDevelopmental noise / bet-hedging
